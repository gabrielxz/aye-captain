CAPTAIN'S HANDBOOK

AYE, CAPTAIN — doctrine for commanding a warship with your voice
← BACK

You command a warship with your voice. Your ship's AI — your XO — translates your orders, runs your systems, and tells you what it sees. It is capable, literal, and unfailingly calm. It does exactly what you say, which is not always what you meant.

This handbook teaches you doctrine. The XO handles the rest. When in doubt about anything — ranges, fuel, what that contact is doing — just ask it.

YOUR FIRST FIVE MINUTES

Start a Practice match — you'll pick your hull and the sparring drone's (want to learn what a Cruiser sounds like? Spar one) — and say these, in order. Hold SPACE to talk (or press Enter and type). On a phone or tablet, hold the 🎙 HOLD button instead.

  1. "Flank speed." — You start accelerating. Note the propellant gauge draining.
  2. "Show me my vector." — A line appears: where you're actually going. Watch it NOT change when you turn. That's space.
  3. "Come to a full stop." — The XO flips the ship and burns until you're still. This is how braking works here. It costs fuel and time.
  4. "Throttle to twenty percent." — Your propellant starts regenerating (only inside the zone, only at low throttle). This is how you refuel: by being patient. The HUD's PROP row shows ⟳ while the ramscoop is harvesting and ✕ when it can't (throttle too high, or you're outside the zone).
  5. "Any contacts?" — The XO reports what the sensors hold. Somewhere out there is a practice drone.
  6. "Point us at the contact." — The helm answers. Now go hunting — the rest of this handbook is how.

DOCTRINE I: SEE WITHOUT BEING SEEN

Detection is the game. Battles are usually decided before the first shot: by who sees whom, first, and how well.

Your ship glows. Not with light — with signature: drive plume, heat, emissions. Your throttle is your signature. At full burn you can be seen from most of the map away. Cruising, from a long way out. Engines cold, drifting silent — you're still there, just seen at a fraction of the distance, and nobody can lock you until they get much closer than that. Dark is an edge, not an off-switch. Firing weapons spikes your signature: a torpedo launch is a flashbulb; even your defensive guns give you away.

READING THE MAP

The dashed circle around your ship is a 50 km ruler — a fixed measuring stick, not a sensor boundary. (Handy coincidence: it's also about the distance a dark drifter gets spotted.) The big ring at the map's edge is the zone boundary. Hover your mouse anywhere and the readout gives you the bearing to that spot — say it to your XO and it becomes an order.

Ask for the drift marker ("show me our drift") and a small chevron rides beside your hull, pointing the way you're going. Your hull points where you are aimed; the drift marker points where you are going. They are not the same thing, and the gap between them is what will kill you. It stays up until you drop it.

What you see of the enemy comes in three grades:

  • FAINT — something's out there. Approximate position, updated occasionally. No vector. You cannot shoot at a faint.
  • TRACK — position and velocity, live. You can get missile lock on a track.
  • ID — close enough to resolve everything, including whether that "ship" was ever a ship at all.

Contacts harden as you close in — or as they burn harder. A patient captain closes dark and quiet; an impatient one lights the sky and hands the enemy a track for free.

Terrain is sensor cover. Rocks block line of sight completely — sensors, locks, missile seekers, everything. Dust clouds blind everyone inside and everything looking through them. The dark behind a rock is the best armor in the game.

Hearing, Seeing, Shooting. Information comes on a ladder. Far beyond sensor range you HEAR ships: a drive rumble — a soft chevron at the edge of your map and a low sound in the hull — giving you a bearing and nothing else. No range, no vector. The louder they run, the farther they carry: a cruising drive is heard across most of the map. Rocks and dust don't muffle it — the shroud carries rumble the way water carries sound. Closer, you SEE ships: the contact tiers above. Closest, you LOCK them. Two bearings taken minutes apart, crossed on the plotting table, are worth more than either alone — that's captain's work; the XO won't do it for you.

Passive and Active. Everything above is passive — you radiate nothing. The alternative is the ping: "give me a ping" snaps everything within range and line of sight to a five-second TRACK — dark ships, decoys, even coasting torpedoes. The price is honest: you are revealed to everyone, map-wide, for ten seconds — watch the red LIT countdown in your HUD while you pay it. A ping finds ships; it cannot hold a lock by itself.

The ping is a circle, not a beam, and it is blocked by rocks and dust like any other sensor. The ring you see expanding on the map is the actual area of effect, and the gaps in it are where terrain ate your ping. A ping that comes back empty with a torn ring didn't prove the sky is clear — it proved you can't see behind those rocks. After your ping, a return blip means a contact — the sooner it comes back, the closer they are. Silence means nothing was out there in the open.

You can be silent, or you can be going somewhere — not both.

One more thing about decoys (fully covered in Doctrine IV): a drifting decoy looks exactly like a distant cruising ship — and it rumbles like one. It draws a designation letter with the same ceremony as a real ship. That "Contact Charlie" you've been stalking for three minutes may be a lie.

DOCTRINE II: MOVE LIKE A NEWTONIAN

Your ship is not an airplane. Turning does not change where you're going — it changes where you're pointing. Velocity only changes when you burn. The helm turns the way you say, the amount you say: "starboard two-seventy" goes the long way around, and yes, you can order a full pirouette.

The helm can hold a target. "Point us at him" — or "keep us pointed at him"; they're the same order — keeps the nose on the contact continuously until you give a new heading. It works on a rumble too ("steer for that rumble" chases the bearing). If the contact fades below faint, the helm holds its last known position and tells you so.

Consequences you must internalize:

  • To stop, you must flip and burn: face backwards along your vector and thrust. "Come to a full stop" makes the XO do the math.
  • Speed is a purchase you must also pay to return. Every m/s you gain, you'll spend fuel to shed.
  • At high speed your turn radius is enormous and rocks are lethal. A gentle bump scratches paint. Hitting rock at speed is a funeral.

Propellant is a budget, not a fuel gauge. A full tank is roughly: accelerate to flank speed once, and kill that speed once. That's it. The tank refills only inside the zone, only while your throttle is at 20% or less — refueling is a posture, and a quiet one (low throttle = low signature; the thrifty ship is also the hidden ship).

Run the tank dry and you drift — you can still turn and shoot, but your vector is no longer yours to choose until the scoop trickles something back.

The edge. Outside the zone ring: everyone can see you, nothing refuels you, and a current pushes you back toward the middle. You can't get stranded out there. You also can't hide, can't stay, and can't win.

Ask the XO anytime: "show me my vector", "how's our fuel?", "what's our speed?"

DOCTRINE III: KILL WITH TORPEDOES

You carry six torpedoes and two launch tubes (they auto-reload; about thirty seconds — fire both and you'll FEEL the wait, so staggered single shots are real doctrine). Torpedoes burn hard, then coast: a violent engine phase — fast, agile, hungry — then the tank runs dry and it's a ballistic round: still deadly on its exact line, blind to your maneuvers, and nearly invisible.

Standoff is part of the weapon: the fuse arms three kilometers from the launch point. Fire inside that and your bird sails straight past without detonating — the XO will warn you if your locked target is too close.

The lock. To fire properly you need missile lock: hold the target near your nose, in range, with a TRACK-grade contact, for about five seconds. The enemy feels your lock — their XO warns them the moment you start painting. The five seconds between "acquiring" and "lock" is the most honest tension in the game: they know it's coming.

Why lock matters — the uplink. A locked torpedo stays wired to your ship: you feed it live tracking as long as you hold the lock, it steers to an intercept point, and it ignores decoys completely — your sensors know a decoy from a hull, and the bird trusts you. Keep the lock, keep the kill.

Lose the lock (they break line of sight, go dark, or you die) and the bird goes autonomous: it hunts with its own small seeker, grabs the brightest thing it can see, and is fully spoofable. It does not re-uplink. Hold your locks.

Blind fire. You can launch without a lock — "put a torpedo down bearing 220" — and the bird rides that bearing hunting for anything bright. Against a dark target it will usually find nothing, or find a decoy. It's not a sniper rifle; it's a flashlight that explodes: flush a hider out of a dust cloud, force their defensive guns to reveal them, deny an escape vector. Six birds total. Spend like it.

And it is fire-and-forget in the honest sense: an autonomous bird carries no telemetry. Unless the end of its flight happens where your own sensors can watch it — in range, line of sight — you will never learn what became of it. A bird that sails beyond your horizon just never phones home. The XO only narrates what the ship can actually see.

(Hover your mouse anywhere on the map: the readout gives you the bearing to that spot. That's your plotting table — and the 50 km ring is your ruler.)

DOCTRINE IV: DON'T DIE

An enemy torpedo is beatable at every stage of its life — with a different tool at each stage.

While they're acquiring: break the geometry. Their lock needs you near their nose, in range, on their sensors as a track. Deny any one of those — turn the engagement, dive behind a rock, go dark before the track hardens.

Against an uplinked bird: decoys are useless — the enemy's sensors see through them. You must break the lock first. Rocks. Dust. Going cold. The instant their lock dies, the bird is orphaned.

Against an autonomous bird: now the decoy works — with one discipline: throttle down first. A decoy is bright, but a full burn is brighter; at high throttle the seeker keeps choosing you. The complete ritual, speakable in one breath:

"Break their lock — throttle down — decoy!"

Against a railgun: any thrust beats a solution. A rail solution assumes you hold constant velocity — a drifting ship is a solved equation. If a rail platform holds a track on you (or you just heard rail fire), keep a trickle of burn on. The slug that was going to kill you misses by the width of your reaction.

Against a ballistic bird: it cannot turn. It kills only along its line. Any lateral burn beats it — but you may not see it coming, because a coasting torpedo is nearly dark. Sometimes the first you'll hear is the XO: "Ballistic inbound, close!" — and then the guns.

The PDCs. Your point-defense cannons are automatic. Posture FREE (the default), they engage any inbound they can see within their bubble — a wall of probability, not a guarantee; salvos leak. Posture HOLD silences them. Why would you ever hold? Because firing PDCs lights you up. A hidden ship whose guns swat a stray torpedo has just told everyone exactly where it is. When you're dark and a bird is passing by that isn't tracking you — do you trust the dark, or trust the guns? That decision is yours, Captain, and it's never comfortable. (They also have limited ammunition, and they will happily waste it defending your decoy.)

Outrun it: no longer a last resort. At range, burning directly away bleeds a torpedo's closure and drains its tank chasing you — genuinely survivable now, and it stacks with the PDC bubble the bird must cross slowly. The ledger: that escape burn costs propellant, and a hard-running drive is heard map-wide. You live, and everyone knows where you went. And at knife range, everyone's PDCs chew everyone's hull. Closing to point blank is a murder-suicide pact; occasionally that's the right play too.

DOCTRINE V: COMMAND, DON'T PILOT

You have one voice and a ship full of systems. The XO closes the gap two ways.

Ask questions. "How far out is he?" "Status of my birds?" "Full report." The XO answers from the ship's actual sensor picture — it will not pretend to know what the sensors don't.

Issue standing orders. Conditional doctrine, stated once, executed the instant it triggers — faster than you ever could:

  • "If a missile comes at us, deploy a decoy and turn hard away."
  • "When you get a track on him, tell me immediately."
  • "If our fuel drops below twenty-five, warn me."
  • "The moment he's in range and on our nose, fire tube one — and keep firing."

Up to six at once. Cancel by name: "belay missile defense." Cancel everything: "belay all." A good captain's standing orders are half the battle fought in advance.

The XO is literal. It interprets ambiguity and tells you what it decided — it will not stop to ask. If it misheard you, that's part of command too. Speak like you mean it.

The XO knows when to shut up. He speaks when he knows something you don't: rejections, delayed completions, threats. Routine acknowledgements of things you can watch on the HUD (throttle, helm, PDCs) stay in the transcript. In a busy fight he batches rumble calls into one line ("Three drives out there, Captain — bearings zero-four-zero, one-eighty, and two-nine-five") and only voices contact changes that can actually hurt you — everything else is logged silently. When you key the mic, he stops talking; only a life-or-death warning finishes, quietly. The XO button in the lobby and topbar sets his verbosity — FULL, TERSE (warnings and news only), or SILENT (warnings only) — and the topbar has separate SFX and VOX volume sliders.

Alarms decay. An enemy lock opens with the full blare, then settles to a soft heartbeat — one thump per ship holding you; a triple-thump means run. Any change (a launch, a new attacker, a re-lock) snaps it back to full. The collision klaxon speeds up as the rock gets closer.

DOCTRINE VI: THE FLEET

Rooms hold up to eight captains — free-for-all or two teams. Everything below is how you fight when the sky has more than one hostile in it.

Three hulls. In multiplayer rooms you pick an archetype — numbers only, no gimmicks. The Corvette is the ghost: quickest, dimmest, the best eyes in the game, six decoys — and no railgun. The Frigate is the baseline everything else is measured against. The Cruiser is the thunderstorm: a hull half again anyone else's, three tubes, deep magazines — and a drive signature that cannot hide, ever. You learn an enemy's hull type only at ID range; until then every contact draws as the same anonymous wedge.

Contacts have names. Your XO designates each hostile track in acquisition order — "Contact Alpha", "Contact Bravo". At ID range the track resolves to the ship's permanent callsign: "Contact Bravo identified: it's Kestrel." Both forms are orders — "point at Bravo", "lock up Kestrel". Lose a track for more than a minute and the XO can't swear the next blip is the same hull: the old fix stays on the map and the new track opens a fresh letter.

The railgun (Frigate and Cruiser): the anti-drifter. A 6 km/s slug on a computed lead kills anything that keeps coasting — and misses anything that burns, because the solution assumes constant velocity. Every posture now has a predator: missiles punish burners, rails punish coasters, PDCs punish missiles. Nothing stops a slug except not being where it lands — not PDCs, and not a transponder: in team games a slug will hit whoever is in the line of fire. If you hear rail fire, burn.

Teams share transponders — and nothing else. Your teammates ride your map at full state, always, in friendly blue. But there is no shared sensor picture: what your wingman sees, you learn when they tell you (teammates are always one tightbeam away). Your guided weapons all check IFF — locks refuse friendlies, seekers and proximity fuses ignore them, your PDCs let your wingman's birds through. The railgun checks nothing. Mind your line of fire.

Talking is a tactical act. A broadcast reaches every captain on the field — and every captain gets a comms chevron with your bearing and your callsign on it. Five seconds of "here I am, and this is my name." A tightbeam is a private whisper with no flare, but you must hold a track on the recipient to point the dish — except teammates, who are always one whisper away. What you say arrives word for word, in your XO's voice on their bridge.

Probes are your remote ears. Launch one down a bearing and it burns, drifts, and relays for three minutes: weak eyes, full hearing, everything marked via probe on your map. The trick that wins hunts: a rumble your ship hears AND your probe hears is two bearing lines — where they cross is your fix. The XO will not cross them for you; that's captain's work. A relayed track can point your nose but never builds a missile lock — probes find ships, your own sensors shoot them.

When you die, you watch. A destroyed captain's XO signs off and the client flows into the spectator view under the ship's callsign. No respawns; the rematch re-seats everyone with the same picks.

Names ride the transponder. Type a name in the lobby and your teammates and the spectators see it; enemies never do — mid-match, everyone hostile is a callsign, and the XO only ever speaks callsigns. The payoff comes at match end: the gameover screen reveals every callsign-to-name mapping and the kill ledger — Vagrant (Marcus) → Kestrel (Gabriel) — and you finally learn who you'd been terrified of for twenty minutes.

Rematch is a ready-up. Your rematch click is a vote (with your same-field / new-field preference); the room relaunches when every captain still connected is ready — majority picks the field, and someone closing their tab never blocks the rest of you. MAIN MENU on the gameover screen — plus the MENU control in practice and spectator views — leaves the room cleanly.

SPECTATING

Any match can be watched. Get the four-letter room code from either captain, enter it in the lobby, and hit WATCH instead of JOIN. You'll be assigned a callsign from the watch roster — Ghost, Echo, Specter and the like — and it's shown on your SPECTATOR badge so the room knows who's who. No name entry, no account; first come, first called.

Spectators see everything: both ships, every torpedo, every decoy — the referee's view, with no fog of war. The captains can't hear you and you can't give orders; the only trace of your presence is a quiet WATCHING readout in the corner of each captain's screen. Fair-play note: that omniscient view means spectating your own opponent's match from a second screen is cheating. Don't be that ghost.

WHEN IN DOUBT

Ask your XO. It knows the ship, it knows the sky, and it never gets tired of questions. The captains who win aren't the ones who memorized this page — they're the ones in constant conversation with their ship.

Good hunting, Captain.
